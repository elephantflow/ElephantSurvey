Towards Generative World Modeling:
A Comprehensive Survey for Novel View Synthesis

Tracing the evolution of scene representation — from geometric reconstruction to generative world models — and positioning Novel View Synthesis as the foundational spatial capability.

ElephantFlow Research
cs.CV cs.LG NeRF · 3DGS · Diffusion World Models · 4D · Embodied AI
Abstract

Novel View Synthesis (NVS) — the task of rendering a scene from arbitrary unseen viewpoints given a set of reference observations — has undergone a paradigm shift from classical geometry-based reconstruction toward learning-driven generative approaches. This survey examines NVS not merely as a rendering technique, but as the foundational spatial capability of Generative World Models: systems that can predict visual scene states under arbitrary conditions, including viewpoint changes, temporal dynamics, and agent interactions. We provide a comprehensive review spanning three dominant paradigms — implicit neural representations (NeRF), explicit Gaussian-based representations (3DGS), and generative model-driven synthesis (diffusion and score distillation methods) — and analyze how each advances, or falls short of, the full demands of world modeling. We further survey emerging 4D approaches that unify spatial and temporal prediction, and identify key open challenges including physical consistency, scalable generalization, and geometry-generation trade-offs. Our analysis reveals that the field is at a critical inflection point where NVS and World Modeling are converging, and lays out a research roadmap toward truly generalizable, physically grounded world models.

Section 1

Introduction

1.1  The Fundamental Challenge

Intelligence, whether biological or artificial, requires the ability to reason about the world beyond what is immediately visible. A surgeon planning an incision must mentally rotate a 3D anatomy from a 2D scan. A self-driving vehicle must anticipate occlusion from a new perspective before rounding a corner. A robot must predict how a grasped object will look from the wrist camera before it reaches the target pose. In each case, the core cognitive operation is the same: synthesizing a plausible visual state of the world at an unobserved vantage point.

This is the problem of Novel View Synthesis (NVS). Formally, given a set of reference images { I1, …, In } captured from known camera poses { π1, …, πn }, the goal is to render a photorealistic image Î at an arbitrary target pose π*, faithfully preserving scene geometry, appearance, and lighting.

While the problem was articulated decades ago in the image-based rendering literature (Levoy & Hanrahan, 1996; McMillan & Bishop, 1995), the past five years have witnessed an extraordinary leap in capability, driven by deep learning, differentiable rendering, and generative modeling. Yet despite remarkable progress, a critical question remains underexplored in existing literature: in what sense does a system that can synthesize novel views "understand" the world? And conversely — what must NVS methods ultimately become in order to serve as the spatial backbone of a true World Model?

This survey is organized around these questions. We treat NVS not as an isolated rendering task, but as the spatial prediction axis of a larger problem: Generative World Modeling.

1.2  What Is a World Model?

Working Definition

A World Model is a learned system that can predict the visual (and optionally physical) state of a scene under arbitrary conditions — including viewpoint changes, temporal evolution, and agent-driven interactions — in a manner that is geometrically consistent, physically plausible, and generalizable beyond the training distribution.

This definition deliberately extends the classic control-theoretic formulation (Ha & Schmidhuber, 2018; LeCun, 2022), where a world model predicts the next latent state given an action. We expand the "condition" axis to include camera pose as a first-class citizen, making NVS a natural special case: when the condition is a viewpoint change and the scene is static, the world model reduces exactly to an NVS system.

Conversely, a world model must go beyond NVS in two critical dimensions:

⏱
Temporal Prediction
A world model must handle dynamic scenes — predicting how the scene evolves over time under physics, agent actions, or autonomous dynamics. Static NVS methods cannot address this axis.
🎮
Action Conditioning
A world model must be controllable: given an agent action (move forward, grasp, rotate), it must render the resulting observation. This requires semantic and physical understanding beyond geometric interpolation.
🌐
Cross-scene Generalization
Per-scene optimization (NeRF, 3DGS) produces scene-specific models. A world model must generalize to unseen environments from as few as a single image or even a text description.

1.3  Why NVS Is the Core of World Modeling

Among all the capabilities that a world model requires, spatial consistency is the foundational constraint. A temporal prediction that is geometrically inconsistent — where a coffee mug occupies two different positions when viewed from two angles — is not a world model; it is a hallucination. NVS is precisely the discipline that enforces multi-view geometric consistency, making it a non-negotiable prerequisite for any credible world model.

Central Thesis: NVS is the spatial inference engine of world modeling. Without it, a "world model" reduces to a video generator — capable of producing plausible-looking sequences but unable to support downstream tasks that require spatial grounding, such as robot manipulation, surgical planning, or autonomous navigation.

This relationship has important implications for research agenda. Advances in NVS — from denser input constraints to sparse-view generalization, from static scenes to dynamic 4D modeling — map directly onto advances in world modeling capability. We use this lens throughout the survey to evaluate the significance of each method: not merely by its PSNR on standard benchmarks, but by its contribution toward the world modeling desiderata outlined in Section 2.

1.4  The Evolution Arc: From Reconstruction to Generation

The history of NVS can be understood as a progressive relaxation of input constraints, each step demanding stronger scene priors and thus moving closer to generative world modeling:

2015–2020
Era I · Classical Reconstruction

Structure-from-Motion (SfM), multi-view stereo, and light field methods dominated. Given dense, well-posed observations, these methods could faithfully reconstruct scene geometry and synthesize novel views via image-based rendering. High-quality outputs were achievable, but only under tightly controlled conditions.

Dense input required; no learning-based prior; brittle to occlusion and reflectance.
2020–2023
Era II · Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF)

Mildenhall et al. (2020) introduced NeRF: a continuous implicit scene representation encoded in an MLP, trained via differentiable volume rendering. The community rapidly extended NeRF to handle unbounded scenes, dynamic deformations, and — critically — cross-scene generalization (pixelNeRF, IBRNet, MVSNeRF). Quality surpassed classical methods by large margins.

Per-scene optimization is slow (hours to days); dynamic scenes remain challenging; no generative prior.
2023–now
Era III · 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS)

Kerbl et al. (2023) replaced the implicit MLP with a collection of explicit anisotropic Gaussian primitives, rendered via differentiable rasterization. Real-time rendering at high fidelity became possible, unlocking interactive applications. The community extended 3DGS to dynamic scenes (4DGS, SpacetimeGaussians) and generalizable feed-forward models (pixelSplat, MVSplat, Splatt3R).

Still reconstruction-centric; generative prior is weak; large-scale open scenes remain difficult.
2022–now
Era IV · Generative Model-Driven NVS

Diffusion models and score distillation methods (DreamFusion, Zero-1-to-3, Cat3D, ReconFusion) introduced powerful visual priors that enable novel view synthesis from single images or text prompts. GAN-based 3D-aware generation (EG3D, pi-GAN) had earlier established this direction. Video diffusion models (Cosmos, Genie 2, ViewCrafter) further unified temporal and viewpoint prediction, arriving at nascent 4D World Models.

Geometry–generation trade-off unresolved; physical consistency not guaranteed; long-horizon coherence lacking.

Each era is not superseded by the next — they coexist, addressing different points on the trade-off frontier between reconstruction fidelity, input efficiency, generalization, and computational cost. The current frontier lies in integrating the geometric precision of NeRF/3DGS with the generative priors of diffusion models, while extending both along the temporal axis. This integration is the path towards Generative World Modeling.

1.5  What Existing NVS Methods Cannot Yet Do

Despite remarkable progress, a systematic gap exists between the current state of NVS and the requirements of a full World Model. We identify four critical dimensions:

Capability Classical NeRF 3DGS Generative NVS 4D World Model (target)
Geometric consistency (multi-view) ✓ ✓ ✓ △ ✓
Sparse / single-image input ✗ △ △ ✓ ✓
Dynamic scene modeling (4D) ✗ △ △ △ ✓
Action / agent controllability ✗ ✗ ✗ △ ✓
Physical plausibility ✗ ✗ ✗ △ ✓
Cross-scene generalization ✗ △ △ ✓ ✓
Real-time rendering △ ✗ ✓ ✗ ✓

✓ = generally achieved   △ = partially achieved or method-dependent   ✗ = not addressed

The table reveals a fundamental insight: no single existing paradigm satisfies all the requirements of a world model simultaneously. Reconstruction-based methods (NeRF, 3DGS) excel at geometric consistency but lack generative priors and action controllability. Generative methods (diffusion-based NVS) provide strong priors but sacrifice geometric rigor. The path to world modeling necessarily involves bridging these paradigms — and this survey traces that bridge.

1.6  Contributions of This Survey

  • 1 A World Modeling Lens for NVS. We introduce a unified analytical framework that evaluates NVS methods not only on standard metrics (PSNR, SSIM, LPIPS) but on their contribution toward the five world modeling desiderata: geometric consistency, generalization, dynamic modeling, physical plausibility, and action controllability.
  • 2 Comprehensive Taxonomy Across Three Paradigms. We systematically survey >150 methods spanning classical reconstruction, NeRF variants, 3DGS variants, and generative NVS approaches, with structured analysis of each method's representation, input regime, rendering mechanism, and world modeling implications.
  • 3 The 4D Convergence Thesis. We articulate and substantiate the claim that the field is converging toward 4D World Modeling — the unification of spatial (NVS) and temporal (video prediction) generation — and trace the concrete technical developments driving this convergence.
  • 4 Open Problems and Research Roadmap. We identify and analyze the unresolved challenges — geometry–generation trade-off, long-horizon temporal consistency, physical grounding, and evaluation methodology — and propose a research roadmap toward generalizable, physically grounded world models.
